### [Op-Ed] AI Agents: From 'Tools' to 'Representatives,' a Revolutionary Evolution of the Interface

The recent discourse in the information technology (IT) industry has shifted beyond mere "generative AI" toward the emergence of "AI Agents." While the craze for Large Language Models (LLMs) triggered by ChatGPT was centered on "assistants" that respond to human queries, we are now entering the era of "representatives" that autonomously set goals, execute tasks, and derive results. This shift is more than just a technical advancement; it is fundamentally shaking the paradigm of the "interface," which is the basic way humans and computers interact.

While conventional software has been a "passive tool" that faithfully executes commands entered by humans, AI agents take "autonomy" as their core driving force. After identifying a user's intent, an agent independently designs the sub-tasks necessary to achieve that goal. For instance, in response to a request to "plan a trip to Jeju Island this weekend," where conventional AI would stop at listing famous attractions, an agent considers the user's budget and preferences to book flights, reserve restaurants, and even prepare alternative routes according to weather changes. In this process, the agent connects with external APIs to perform "actions" such as actual payments or reservations.

At the center of this transformation lies the concept of the "Agentic Workflow." This refers to a structure designed to let AI go through an iterative cognitive process of "planning, executing, critiquing, and correcting," rather than expecting it to produce a perfect result at once. This is similar to the process where a skilled planner drafts a proposal and then reviews and supplements it themselves. Through this, "hallucination"—a chronic problem of AI—can be reduced, and the success rate in tasks requiring complex reasoning can be dramatically increased.

From an economic perspective, the proliferation of AI agents signifies the birth of a labor force with a "marginal cost near zero." Corporations can maximize productivity by delegating everything from simple repetitive tasks to high-level data analysis to agents. However, where the light is bright, the shadow is also deep. The ambiguity of legal and ethical responsibility that arises as agents act on behalf of human decision-making is an urgent task we must resolve. If an agent causes financial loss or leaks personal information due to an erroneous judgment, who holds the responsibility: the developer, the user, or the algorithm itself?

Furthermore, the "disappearance of the interface" demands a change in human cognitive abilities. As we transition from an era of manipulating icons on a screen via mouse and keyboard to an era of obtaining results through language and intent alone, the competency required of humans is shifting. It is moving from "how to perform (How)" to the ability to plan and think critically—defining "what to do and why (What & Why)."

Ultimately, the advent of the AI agent era urges humans to utilize a higher level of intelligence. As technology becomes our hands and feet, humans must sharpen their insight as a "helmsman" who determines the direction in which that technology should move. While the agent wanders through the complex maze of execution, it is time for us to ask ourselves whether we possess the vision to clearly see the destination beyond that maze.
