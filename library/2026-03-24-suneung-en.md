**[Analysis] Energy as the Fuel for Innovation: Why the AI Era is Returning to 'Nuclear Power'**

While the advancement of generative Artificial Intelligence (AI) has recently become a central theme in the global IT industry, a formidable practical barrier—securing energy—lies beneath the surface. The phenomenon of global Big Tech companies, such as Microsoft, Google, and Amazon, investing trillions of won to sign direct contracts with nuclear power plants or venturing into the development of Small Modular Reactors (SMRs) symbolically illustrates a drastic shift in the modern industrial structure. To understand this, it is necessary to closely examine the characteristics of AI technology and the structural contradictions within energy supply systems.

The training and inference processes of AI consume dozens of times more electricity than conventional cloud services. Data centers equipped with the latest GPUs are often referred to as "electricity-devouring hippos," requiring an uninterrupted power supply 24 hours a day. This is where the concept of "base load" emerges. Renewable energy sources, such as solar or wind power, suffer from high variability depending on weather conditions, making it difficult to meet the demands of data centers that require a constant and stable power supply. In contrast, nuclear power, which produces large-scale electricity stably with almost no carbon emissions, has resurfaced as a critical alternative.

However, traditional large-scale nuclear power plants are constrained by astronomical construction costs, prolonged construction periods, and the immense risks associated with potential accidents. In this context, Small Modular Reactors (SMRs) have emerged as a viable alternative. SMRs are small-scale reactors with an electrical output of 300 megawatts (MW) or less, designed to integrate key components into a single modular vessel for on-site assembly. This approach allows for a radical reduction in construction time and significant cost savings.

From a technical perspective, the most prominent feature of SMRs is the implementation of "passive safety systems." This method utilizes physical laws, such as gravity or natural convection, to circulate coolant and cool the reactor even in emergency situations where the power supply is cut off. The logic is to fundamentally eliminate the possibility of accidents without the need for human intervention. Furthermore, SMRs offer greater flexibility in site selection compared to large-scale plants; by being built directly near data centers, they can minimize power transmission costs and energy loss.

Nevertheless, SMRs do not guarantee a purely optimistic future. Technical maturity is still being verified, and the inherent challenge of nuclear energy—radioactive waste disposal—remains unresolved. Moreover, despite their advantages as "distributed power sources," the issue of social acceptance regarding the construction of reactors near residential areas is likely to be the most significant hurdle in the future commercialization process.

Ultimately, the sustainability of the AI industry depends not merely on the sophistication of algorithms, but on how efficiently and safely the energy to drive those algorithms can be secured. The current SMR craze is a classic example of how technological innovation forces a shift in the energy paradigm. While we enjoy the benefits of cutting-edge technology, we must not cease our critical reflection on energy justice and environmental responsibility.
