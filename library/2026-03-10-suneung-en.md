# [Editorial] From Tools to Subjects: How 'AI Agents' Are Reshaping the Digital Order

The central discourse in the information technology (IT) industry is rapidly shifting / from simple "Generative AI" that provides answers / to "AI Agents" that judge and execute tasks autonomously. While past artificial intelligence remained in the role of an "encyclopedia" / that summarized and delivered knowledge in response to human queries, / it has now entered the stage of an "agent" / that understands the user’s goals, establishes complex execution plans, and manipulates external tools. This technological inflection point is prompting fundamental questions / about the economic structure of our society and the definition of human labor.

The core momentum of AI agents lies in "autonomy" and "execution capability." While existing Large Language Models (LLMs) focused on generating sentences through the probabilistic combination of text, / an agent system is completed through the integration of three elements: reasoning, planning, and tool use. For instance, if a user gives a command like "Book a trip to Jeju Island for next week," / the agent retrieves the user’s preferences from memory, searches for real-time flight and accommodation data, and finally completes the task / by linking with payment systems. In this process, AI is elevated beyond a mere language model / to an active subject that exerts substantial influence within the digital environment.

From an economic perspective, the emergence of AI agents is shaking the paradigm of the "Software as a Service (SaaS)" market. Previously, users had to learn how to operate various software themselves; / however, AI agents are now breaking down the barriers between software / and controlling interfaces on behalf of humans. This represents an opportunity to drastically increase corporate productivity, / while simultaneously posing a threat / that could rapidly devalue simple, repetitive clerical labor. Experts define this as the "automation of cognitive labor" / and analyze that the human role will shift from "execution" to "supervision and final decision-making."

However, behind this technological optimism, unresolved challenges remain scattered. The most significant issues are "controllability" and the "locus of responsibility." There is a lack of social consensus / regarding who should be held legally and ethically accountable / when an autonomous agent makes an unexpected error or acts contrary to the user's intention. Furthermore, the issue of data privacy infringement, / which arises as AI learns every detail of an individual's life to serve as a secretary, / is a prerequisite task that must be resolved in the era of agents.

Ultimately, the arrival of the AI agent era requires a new form of "digital literacy" from humans. It has become important / not just to know how to use technology, / but how to design and monitor the autonomy of that technology. Behind the convenience of artificial intelligence serving as our hands and feet / lies the heavy responsibility that we must not stop thinking independently. Amidst the waves of rapidly changing technology, / it is time to reflect once again on what human values we must preserve.
