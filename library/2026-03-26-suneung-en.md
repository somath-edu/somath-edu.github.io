**[Senior Editor’s Perspective] The Evolution of Generative AI: From Probabilistic Prediction to the Realm of Logical Thought**

The most prominent topic in the recent artificial intelligence (AI) industry is the emergence of "reasoning AI," which moves beyond simply answering questions to "thinking and self-correcting." While the Large Language Models (LLMs) like ChatGPT, which have swept the world over the past few years, operated by predicting the most probable next word based on vast amounts of data, newly released next-generation models have begun to equip themselves with "thought systems" that follow internal logical steps to solve complex problems.

Psychologist Daniel Kahneman categorized human thought systems into two types: "System 1," which is intuitive and responds rapidly, and "System 2," which is logical and deliberates analytically. If traditional AI provided immediate answers in a manner closer to "System 1," the latest reasoning models are evaluated to have entered the domain of "System 2," verifying their own logic through the "Chain of Thought" technique.

The core of this technological leap lies in reinforcement learning. In the process of problem-solving, AI explores numerous paths leading to the correct answer and learns for itself which path is the most efficient and accurate. During this process, AI acquires the ability to discover and correct its own errors. This serves as a key to dramatically reducing "hallucination"—the phenomenon where AI presents false information as if it were a fact—which has been a chronic problem of existing AI.

However, the implications of these changes for our society do not stop at mere technological progress. Experts predict that reasoning AI will significantly replace or assist human roles in professional fields that require high-level logical thinking, such as coding, mathematics, and scientific research. While AI replaced simple repetitive tasks in the past, now even "deep thinking," once considered a unique domain of human intelligence, is being incorporated into the realm of machines.

Ultimately, in the approaching future, "critical thinking skills"—the ability to verify the validity of the "reasoning process" made by AI and to ask the right questions—will be demanded of humans more than the ability to simply accept the "answers" provided by AI. In an era where artificial intelligence has begun to think like a human, we stand at a critical inflection point where we must redefine what constitutes intelligence unique to humanity.
