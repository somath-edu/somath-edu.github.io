**[Lead] Artificial Intelligence (AI) is moving beyond mere technological advancement to fundamentally reshape the global industrial landscape. At the heart of this shift lies the growth of NVIDIA, which symbolizes a structural transition in how humanity processes data and generates value.**

The focus of global financial markets and industries has shifted decisively toward "AI semiconductors." Just as coal and oil were the driving forces of the Industrial Revolution, data has become the "crude oil" of the AI era, with the Graphics Processing Unit (GPU) serving as the engine that refines this data into value. Within this landscape, NVIDIA occupies an unrivaled position, capturing more than 80% of the global AI accelerator market.

The technical foundation of this dominance lies in the concept of "parallel computing." While a traditional Central Processing Unit (CPU) is specialized for processing complex instructions sequentially, a GPU is optimized for handling massive amounts of simple calculations simultaneously using thousands of cores. Given the nature of the AI industry, which requires calculating trillions of parameters to train Large Language Models (LLMs), GPU-based accelerated computing has become an indispensable necessity rather than a choice.

However, NVIDIA’s true competitive advantage lies in its software ecosystem known as "CUDA." By establishing a programming platform that runs exclusively on its own hardware, NVIDIA has maximized a "lock-in effect," making global developers dependent on its environment. From an economic perspective, this functions as a powerful "moat" that raises entry barriers for latecomers by creating high switching costs.

The economic implication of this phenomenon is the "assetization of computing resources." Data centers are no longer mere warehouses for storing information; they are transforming into "AI factories" that produce intelligence in real-time. Corporations are competing to secure computing power through astronomical capital investments to provide faster AI services, leading to an explosive increase in semiconductor demand and a reorganization of global supply chains.

Admittedly, optimism is not the only prevailing sentiment. Overdependence on a single company increases supply chain risks, and concerns regarding environmental issues from massive power consumption and potential "AI bubbles" are also rising. Nevertheless, what we must observe is how innovation that has surpassed a technological threshold disrupts the existing economic order and creates new added value.

Students should use this text to understand the correlation between technological shifts—moving from hardware to software ecosystems—and economic decision-making. It is necessary to practice logically connecting how the keywords "efficiency" and "pre-emption" establish a monopolistic status in the market.

**[Senior Editor’s Comment]**
"Ultimately, future hegemony depends not on who possesses the most data, but on who owns the infrastructure capable of converting that data into 'intelligence' at the highest speed."
