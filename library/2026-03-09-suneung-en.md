# From Tool to Proxy: The Dawn of the AI Agent Era

The focal point of the global IT industry is rapidly shifting beyond "Generative AI," which simply responds to inquiries, toward "AI Agents" that perceive user intentions and execute tasks autonomously. While previous artificial intelligence focused on "linguistic play"—generating sophisticated sentences or images based on vast datasets—it has now entered the "stage of execution," directly controlling computer operating systems (OS) and completing complex workflows. This shift signals a fundamental change in the paradigm of human-computer interaction, transcending mere technological advancement.

What exactly is an AI agent? While conventional Large Language Models (LLMs) functioned as passive encyclopedias, agents possess the autonomy to formulate plans, select necessary tools, and revise their paths based on execution results to achieve a goal. For instance, in response to the command "Plan a trip to Jeju Island," where traditional AI would merely list restaurants and tourist attractions, an AI agent would check the user's credit card information and schedule, book flight tickets, reserve accommodations, and even negotiate with car rental companies. This transition signifies that AI has been elevated from a "tool" to a "proxy."

The backdrop of this technological leap is the advancement of cognitive systems known as "agentic workflows." This method increases accuracy by allowing the AI to undergo a process of "reflection," in which it reviews and modifies its own outputs rather than deriving an answer all at once. Furthermore, as "tool use" capabilities—the ability to freely manipulate external APIs or software—have been strengthened, AI has finally become able to cross the physical and digital barriers of the real world.

However, the emergence of these "autonomous proxies" poses significant ethical and economic questions to our society. The first is the issue of the "locus of responsibility." If an AI agent makes an incorrect financial payment or violates a legal contract while performing a user's instruction, does the responsibility lie with the developer or the user who gave the command? Since existing legal systems are designed on the premise of human free will, there is still a lack of clear grounds for compensating for damages caused by the autonomous judgment of AI.

The second issue involves changes in the labor market. While past automation replaced simple, repetitive physical labor, AI agents can directly perform core "white-collar" tasks such as planning, analysis, report writing, and schedule management. This could maximize the efficiency of highly skilled professionals, but conversely, it raises concerns that it may lead to the rapid disappearance of jobs requiring mid-level expertise.

Ultimately, the attitude required of us in the era of AI agents is not blind trust or vague fear, but rather "critical acceptance and control." We must develop the insight to monitor whether the process is being managed transparently when AI acts on our behalf. It is time to meticulously design ethical guidelines and safety devices from the technical design stage to ensure that technology does not threaten human agency while relieving human toil. AI no longer stays within the screen; social consensus for coexistence with AI, which has become the "hand" that moves our daily lives, is urgent.
