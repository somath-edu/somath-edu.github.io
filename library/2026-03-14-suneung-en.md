## [Opinion] The Invasion of AI and the Economics of Energy: Can We Endure the "Electricity Famine"?

The most prominent topic resonating throughout the global community recently is undoubtedly the evolution of generative artificial intelligence (AI). AI, which now freely generates video and audio beyond mere text, is being hailed as a "magical tool" that will drastically enhance human productivity. However, behind the glamour of this technology lies a profound logical contradiction known as the "AI Paradox." As astronomical amounts of power are consumed to manifest high-level intelligence, the world is facing an unprecedented energy crisis.

Statistics reveal a stark contrast in energy consumption: while a standard Google search consumes approximately 0.3 watt-hours (Wh) of electricity, a single interaction with a generative AI like ChatGPT consumes about 2.9 Wh—nearly ten times as much. The learning and inference processes of AI are based on data centers where tens of thousands of Graphics Processing Units (GPUs) are connected in parallel. These devices operate 24 hours a day, emitting immense heat, and the cooling systems required to dissipate this heat also devour massive amounts of electricity. In essence, we have entered a structure where the more we desire "smarter" AI, the faster the depletion of Earth’s energy resources becomes.

This phenomenon is causing a shift in economic and geopolitical paradigms that extends beyond simple environmental concerns. While Big Tech companies previously focused on competing through software algorithms, the core of survival has now shifted to "who can secure more stable power." This shift is evidenced by moves from companies like Google and Microsoft to sign direct contracts with nuclear power plants, including Small Modular Reactors (SMRs), or even to construct their own power plants near data centers. Technological hegemony is moving beyond the excellence of algorithms toward the dominance of energy infrastructure.

From a critical reading perspective, the point we must notice is the "externalization of costs." The enormous profits enjoyed by AI companies may be the result of transferring environmental costs and cheap electricity burdens to society as a whole. The surge in power demand leads to increases in electricity bills, placing a burden on general households, while rising carbon emissions accelerate the climate crisis. The traditional economic belief that technological progress necessarily increases the utility of the entire society has hit a realistic constraint called the "energy bottleneck."

Ultimately, the sustainability of the AI era depends on the integration of two pillars: algorithmic efficiency and energy innovation. The development of low-power semiconductors and the transition to renewable energy are no longer choices but essential conditions for survival. Before we become enthralled by the convenience brought by AI, we must soberly calculate the "weight of energy" that must be paid to maintain that intelligence. If the advancement of intelligence does not guarantee a sustainable future for humanity, that technology may eventually end up being nothing more than a mirage in the middle of a desert.
