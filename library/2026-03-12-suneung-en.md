**[Analytical Report] Beyond Probabilistic Generation to the Era of 'Reasoning': Qualitative Leaks in LLMs and Impending Changes**

The trajectory of the generative artificial intelligence (AI) market is currently facing a sharp turning point. While large language models (LLMs), represented by ChatGPT, have traditionally focused on "linguistic fluency"—predicting the most probable next word based on vast amounts of data—the core topic has now shifted to "reasoning capability," the ability to take logical steps to solve problems. Recent models have demonstrated performance nearing that of human experts in complex mathematics, coding, and scientific hypothesis testing, suggesting that AI is evolving beyond a simple assistive tool into an independent thinking agent.

Traditional LLMs tended to rely heavily on "System 1 thinking." This concept, defined by psychologist Daniel Kahneman, refers to intuitive and immediate responses. In contrast, recent technological advancements represent an attempt to implant "System 2 thinking"—a slow, deliberate, and logical thought process—into AI. The key mechanism enabling this is the combination of "Chain of Thought (CoT)" techniques and reinforcement learning. Before providing an answer, the AI generates intermediate reasoning steps, reviews errors, and explores the optimal path. This principle is similar to how a student writes down a step-by-step solution on a scratchpad to reach the correct answer to a difficult math problem.

This enhancement in reasoning power heralds disruptive innovations across the entire economy and industry. While previous AI was limited to standardized tasks such as writing emails or simple summarization, "reasoning AI" is penetrating fields that require high levels of expertise and logical judgment, such as legal analysis, drug discovery, and precision engineering. From a corporate perspective, this means being able to receive AI assistance in high-value-added decision-making processes, rather than just increasing work speed. This is expected to accelerate structural changes in the labor market, demanding that humans possess stronger capabilities as "high-level architects" who can identify logical flaws in AI or ask creative questions.

However, the outlook is not entirely optimistic. As reasoning capabilities become more sophisticated, the "black box" problem—where it becomes difficult for humans to fully understand the internal logical process through which an AI reached a conclusion—deepens. Furthermore, the increase in computational power required for logical reasoning processes creates an economic burden in the form of massive power consumption and computing costs. This is why establishing "AI governance" is urgent, to verify whether the logical conclusions presented by AI are ethically sound or are sophisticated errors based on biased data.

Ultimately, the emergence of "thinking AI" poses a new question to humanity. When machines begin to perform "rational reasoning," which was once considered a unique domain of humans, where should human intellectual value be found? We are now faced with a point where we must move beyond simple collaboration with AI and acquire a new level of literacy to critically accept and control the AI's thinking process. Now more than ever, we need an insightful eye to perceive the structural changes hidden behind the abundance brought by technological progress.
