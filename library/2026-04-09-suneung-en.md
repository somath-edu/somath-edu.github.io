### [Opinion] The Intelligence of AI Derived from Electricity: The Energy Paradox and the Inevitability of SMRs

The recent rapid advancement of generative Artificial Intelligence (AI) is fundamentally reshaping the landscape of human existence. However, behind the veil of sophisticated algorithms lies a stringent physical constraint known as "energy gluttony." Data centers designed to operate Large Language Models (LLMs), such as ChatGPT, consume dozens of times more electricity than conventional data centers. As the world accelerates to seize dominance in AI, a prevailing analysis suggests that the success of this "digital revolution" ironically depends not on software, but on hardware—specifically, a stable supply of power.

This situation gives rise to what is called the "AI Energy Paradox." As the volume of computation increases to enhance intelligence, the required amount of electricity grows exponentially; yet, relying on fossil fuels to meet this demand conflicts with the international norm of "carbon neutrality." Renewable energy sources, such as solar and wind, are insufficient to support the "base load" of data centers—which must operate 24/7 without interruption—due to their high variability based on weather conditions. Amidst this dilemma, Small Modular Reactors (SMRs), the next generation of nuclear power, have emerged as a compelling alternative for global Big Tech companies.

SMRs refer to a type of reactor that reduces the power generation capacity to about one-third of existing large-scale nuclear plants while integrating major components into a single module. The reasons why SMRs have stepped up as the "relief pitcher" of the AI era are clear. First is safety. SMRs have drastically lowered the risk of accidents by adopting passive cooling systems that can cool the reactor core through natural circulation without the need for boron water injection or cooling pumps. Second is locational flexibility. Since they do not require the vast amounts of cooling water that large plants do, they can be constructed near data centers, which becomes a key advantage in reducing power transmission grid costs and energy loss.

In fact, global IT giants such as Microsoft and Amazon have recently been entering into power supply agreements with nuclear companies or engaging in direct investments. This suggests that AI competitiveness has moved beyond a mere battle of data and algorithms to a stage where it is directly linked to energy security. Now, electricity is no longer a secondary element of economic growth but has become a "strategic asset" that determines the survival of nations and corporations.

Of course, many hurdles remain for the commercialization of SMRs. Verifying technical maturity, addressing nuclear waste disposal, and securing public acceptance in local communities are ongoing challenges. However, amidst the irresistible wave of AI, the demand for power is skyrocketing, and a stable, carbon-free power source has become a necessity rather than an option. We are currently standing at an unprecedented intersection where the expansion of digital intelligence mandates a fundamental transformation of physical energy infrastructure. To enjoy the prosperity brought by technological progress, cool-headed insight and social consensus regarding the underlying energy system are more urgent than ever.
