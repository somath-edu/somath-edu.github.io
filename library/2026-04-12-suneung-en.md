# [Opinion] When the Heart of AI Enters Your Palm: The Rise and Challenges of On-Device AI

Recently, the paradigm of Artificial Intelligence (AI) technology has been shifting from massive cloud servers to individual terminals, marking the dawn of the "On-Device AI" era. Until now, generative AI—represented by ChatGPT—has primarily adopted a cloud-based approach, where vast amounts of data are processed on central servers. However, technology that enables devices to perform inference and computation themselves by embedding high-performance computing units directly into smartphones, laptops, and home appliances is rapidly spreading. This represents a fundamental change that shakes the structure of the data economy and the paradigm of personal information protection beyond mere technological progress.

The core driver of On-Device AI is the rapid advancement of Neural Processing Units (NPUs). While traditional Central Processing Units (CPUs) or Graphics Processing Units (GPUs) are optimized for general-purpose computing, NPUs are specifically designed to efficiently process complex neural network operations, much like the human brain. Through this, devices can perform real-time translation, photo enhancement, and personalized assistant functions without an external network connection. As network latency disappears, response speeds have increased dramatically, and this has become a dominant trend in the industry, aligning with the interests of companies seeking to reduce server operation costs.

The most noteworthy point is the establishment of "data sovereignty." A chronic problem with cloud-based AI was the security vulnerability that occurred during the process of transmitting sensitive personal information to external servers. On-Device AI fundamentally blocks the possibility of information leakage by processing all data only within the device (Local Processing). This serves as an opportunity to dispel concerns about a digital surveillance society and to strengthen "self-determination," where users possess full control over their own data.

However, On-Device AI does not only promise a rosy future. Due to the hardware limitations of individual devices, the size of the models is inevitably smaller compared to the cloud-based approach, which may reveal limitations in complex logical reasoning or vast knowledge retrieval. Furthermore, the rise in device prices due to high-performance chipsets, heat management, and the optimization of battery consumption are tasks that manufacturers must solve. Most importantly, if the "trained models" stored inside the device become targets for hacking, the risk of leaking profiling information that contains personal tastes and habits cannot be ruled out.

Ultimately, the success of On-Device AI depends on the balance between technological sophistication and ethical safety nets. We are entering an era where AI is no longer the property of distant tech giants but a personal tool operating in our hands. It is a time when we need an attitude of reflecting on the weight of data hidden behind the stimulating values of efficiency and convenience. Although technology exists for human convenience, the rules of the world that technology creates must be established through human critical thinking.
