**[Report] Evolution Beyond the Cloud: The New Paradigm Drawn by On-Device AI**

Recently, the most prominent topic in the Information and Communication Technology (ICT) industry is undoubtedly "On-Device AI." Unlike conventional generative AI, which functioned only by passing through servers in massive data centers, On-Device AI refers to a method where data is processed directly through chipsets embedded within the device itself. This is evaluated as a shift that goes beyond mere technological progress, changing the fundamental grammar of how humanity utilizes digital devices.

The core driver of On-Device AI is the exponential advancement of Neural Processing Units (NPUs). While processors in past smartphones or laptops focused on calculation speeds and graphic processing, modern chipsets are optimized to efficiently execute the complex matrix operations of artificial intelligence. Consequently, high-level tasks such as real-time translation, photo editing, and document summarization can be performed instantaneously within the device without accessing vast cloud servers.

The primary advantages brought by this shift can be summarized into three categories. First is "security"; as sensitive personal information is processed within the device rather than being transmitted to external servers, concerns over data leakage are significantly reduced. Second is "low latency"; by eliminating the physical delay that occurs during communication with servers, near-instantaneous response speeds can be realized. Third is "cost and energy efficiency"; the enormous power consumption and server operation costs incurred when millions of users access servers can be drastically diminished.

However, the proliferation of On-Device AI does not only promise a rosy future. Because AI performance is determined by the hardware specifications of the device itself, there is a risk that the so-called "digital divide" may become entrenched depending on hardware ownership. Furthermore, the performance degradation that occurs during the process of downsizing Large Language Models (LLMs) for mobile devices with limited battery and computational resources—specifically issues of "information volatility" or "decreased accuracy"—remains a challenge to be solved.

From an economic perspective, On-Device AI is reshaping the competition for dominance in the semiconductor market. Following the server-side AI chip market previously led by NVIDIA, global giants such as Samsung Electronics, Apple, and Qualcomm are now engaged in a fierce "Edge Computing" war over the AI chip market for smartphones and PCs. This signifies an entry into the era of "Phygital" (Physical + Digital) convergence, where the software-centered AI ecosystem merges once again with hardware innovation.

Ultimately, the normalization of On-Device AI will hasten the era where artificial intelligence becomes a "constant companion" that assists human intellectual activities rather than just a specialized tool. We have passed the stage of asking what AI can do, and we are now facing the fundamental question of how humans will ethically and creatively control the intelligence inherent within their devices.
