**[Analysis] The Birth of 'Reasoning AI' and Structural Fluctuations in the Knowledge Economy**

The trajectory of recent advancements in artificial intelligence (AI) is taking a sharp turn from mere "information assembly" toward "logical inference." While generative AI, represented by ChatGPT, previously focused on predicting the most probable next word based on vast datasets, latest models have begun to implement "Chain of Thought" techniques—a process where the AI independently establishes and verifies logical steps to solve problems. This implies the arrival of the "Era of Reasoning," where the competition is no longer about the speed of providing an answer, but rather about the quality of the process used to reach it.

Psychologist Daniel Kahneman famously demarcated the human thinking system into "System 1," which is intuitive and fast, and "System 2," which is logical and deliberate. While existing Large Language Models (LLMs) relied on System 1-like mechanisms to generate instantaneous responses, newer reasoning AI mimics the deep thinking processes of System 2 to tackle complex mathematical problems, coding, and sophisticated logical structures. This process—in which AI corrects its own errors and explores the optimal logical path—demonstrates that machines have entered the realm of critical thinking, once considered the exclusive domain of higher human intelligence.

These technological strides pose fundamental questions to the knowledge labor market and the field of education. If past knowledge work focused on collecting fragmented information and processing it within fixed frameworks, those roles are now highly likely to be entirely replaced by AI. As AI becomes capable of "reasoning" on its own, the core competency required of humans is shifting: from the ability to find answers to the "ability to define the right problems" and the "capacity to discern the validity of the logical processes" derived by AI.

From an economic perspective, the proliferation of reasoning AI will drastically lower the marginal cost of knowledge production. This is expected to trigger an unprecedented efficiency revolution in fields previously classified as high-value industries, such as legal counseling, strategic planning, and software design. Simultaneously, however, this carries the inherent risk of accelerating "skill-biased technological change" caused by the automation of intelligence. A wider disparity may emerge between a minority of high-level knowledge workers who utilize AI as a tool for complex decision-making and the majority of workers whose routine tasks are displaced by AI.

Ultimately, in the approaching future, reading comprehension and logical thinking skills are set to become essential mechanisms for survival rather than mere tools for entrance exams. To identify logical flaws in AI-generated text and to exercise uniquely human insights that machines cannot replicate, the capacity for critical reading—analyzing the intentions and structures hidden beneath the text—is more important than ever. We are currently standing at a historical inflection point where AI has begun to "think," and the very definition of intelligence is being redefined.
