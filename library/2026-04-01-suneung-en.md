### [Editorial] From Generation to Reasoning: The Qualitative Leap and Challenges of the AI Paradigm

The recent focus of the artificial intelligence (AI) industry is shifting from mere "speed" in providing answers to "reasoning" capabilities, which involve self-checking the process of reaching a conclusion. The emergence of OpenAI’s new model, ‘o1,’ is regarded as an attempt to overcome the limitations of existing Large Language Models (LLMs) and to transplant "System 2"—the human cognitive system—into machines. This signifies a major inflection point for overall human intellectual activity, extending far beyond the development of the IT industry.

Existing LLMs relied on a method of statistically predicting the next word based on vast amounts of data. To explain this using Daniel Kahneman’s terminology, it is similar to "System 1" thinking, which is immediate and intuitive. While this method secured linguistic fluency, it inherently carried the "hallucination phenomenon," where the model produces erroneous answers when solving complex logical operations or scientific challenges. This is because statistical probability does not necessarily guarantee logical validity.

In contrast, recently developed reasoning-specialized models have internalized a technique called the "Chain of Thought." Before providing an answer, the model goes through a process of decomposing the problem into steps, reviewing intermediate stages, and correcting errors. This is the implementation of "System 2" thinking, which is slow but deliberate and logical. In particular, these models are trained through reinforcement learning—giving penalties for wrong answers and rewards for logical paths leading to the correct one—resulting in overwhelming performance improvements in fields requiring rigorous logic, such as mathematics, coding, and physics.

This shift is also expected to change the economic grammar of the AI industry. Until now, the core focus was on investing massive computational resources into the "training" phase of creating models. However, in the era of reasoning models, the proportion of computation in the "inference" phase—where the model answers user questions—is increasing rapidly. The "Inference-time Scaling Law," which suggests that the quality of intelligence improves in proportion to the time spent "deliberating" before outputting an answer, is emerging as a new standard.

However, these technological advances do not guarantee only a rosy future. There are concerns that the massive power consumption and computational costs required for the reasoning process may lower the economic accessibility of AI. Furthermore, as AI acquires logical validity, the "black box" problem—where it becomes difficult for humans to fully understand the basis of AI's judgments—could further intensify. Above all, we must listen to social warnings that human critical thinking functions might atrophy when AI begins to replace the realm of "deliberation," which was once considered a unique human domain.

Ultimately, the advent of "reasoning AI" poses questions beyond the evolution of a tool. As technology mimics human thought processes, paradoxically, the weight of "value judgment" and "ethical responsibility"—which only humans can provide—will inevitably become heavier. We now stand at a point where we must prepare a higher level of wisdom to set the direction in which logic should aim, moving beyond the ability to simply verify the logic of the answers provided by AI.
