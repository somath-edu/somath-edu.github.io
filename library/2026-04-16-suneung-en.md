**[Analysis] The Transformation of AI’s Thought System and New Challenges for Society**

The most prominent topic in the recent information technology (IT) industry is undoubtedly 'o1,' the new model announced by OpenAI. While previous generative AI models were characterized as 'language models' that predicted the most probable next word based on vast amounts of data, this newly emerged model focuses on 'reasoning' capabilities, which involve thinking step-by-step like a human. This suggests that artificial intelligence is evolving beyond a mere repository of knowledge into an agent of high-level logical development.

Psychologist Daniel Kahneman categorized human thought systems into two types: 'System 1,' which is intuitive and responds quickly, and 'System 2,' which is logical and approaches problems cautiously. Existing Large Language Models (LLMs) have primarily relied on System 1. Although they provide instantaneous answers to user queries, they have frequently exposed vulnerabilities when faced with complex mathematical problems or sophisticated logical puzzles. The 'hallucination phenomenon' occurring in this process has been pointed out as a chronic problem that undermines the reliability of AI.

However, recent technological leaps attempt to overcome this through the 'Chain of Thought' technique. The new model undergoes a process of setting intermediate steps for itself and reviewing the validity of each step before providing an answer. In other words, rather than outputting results immediately, the AI takes internal 'thinking time' to correct errors and explore the optimal logical path. This means that AI has begun to make strategic judgments for problem-solving, rather than merely listing information.

The emergence of such 'reasoning AI' is expected to have a dual impact on society as a whole. On the positive side, innovative productivity improvements can be expected in fields requiring high levels of expertise, such as scientific research, coding, and legal analysis. There is a high possibility that AI will establish itself as a core research partner beyond a mere human assistant in processes like drug development, which must consider thousands of variables, or the proof of complex physical laws.

Nevertheless, there are significant voices of concern. As the reasoning process of AI becomes longer, it consumes massive computing resources and electricity, which leads directly to rising service costs and environmental burdens. Furthermore, as AI begins to construct logical causal relationships on its own, the 'black-box' phenomenon—where it becomes difficult for humans to fully understand or control the process of that thought—may intensify. If an AI’s conclusion, which appears logically perfect, starts from biased data or incorrect premises, it becomes even more difficult for humans to verify it.

Ultimately, the progress of technology is demanding a new form of literacy from us. Moving beyond the level of simply reading and understanding text, 'digital critical thinking'—the ability to identify flaws in the logical reasoning processes presented by AI and accept them critically—is emerging as a core competency. In an era where AI 'thinks,' what is paradoxically most needed will be the power of uniquely human sharp questioning and reflection, which avoids being submerged in the logic of the machine.

**[Lead Reporter's Comment]**
If AI has begun to contemplate like a human, humans must doubt more deeply than AI. As the speed of technology accelerates, what we must hold onto is, ultimately, the 'initiative of thought.'
