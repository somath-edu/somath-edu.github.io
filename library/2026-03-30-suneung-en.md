**[Lead] As the wave of the Fourth Industrial Revolution triggered by Artificial Intelligence (AI) accelerates, the world is facing an unprecedented "energy thirst." While past industrial revolutions blossomed upon fossil fuels such as coal and oil, the modern digital revolution thrives on vast amounts of "electricity." Now, the core of technological hegemony is shifting beyond mere algorithmic competition toward the issue of "energy security"—specifically, how stably and efficiently power can be supplied to support these advancements.**

The recent proliferation of generative AI has led to an explosive expansion of data centers. Large Language Models (LLMs) operated by companies like Google and OpenAI consume immense power not only during the training phase but also during the inference process, where they respond to user queries. In fact, generating a single response from ChatGPT is known to consume about ten times more electricity than a standard Google search. This implies that data centers have evolved beyond mere information storage facilities into "power black holes" that devour massive amounts of energy.

The problem is that current power supply systems are insufficient to handle such a rapid surge in demand. Data centers require a "baseload" characteristic, meaning they need a constant and steady supply of power 24 hours a day. However, renewable energy sources like solar and wind, which are increasing in share to achieve carbon neutrality, have limitations in providing a stable supply due to their inherent variability based on weather conditions. Herein lies the "power paradox": as we cultivate AI to lead in advanced technology, ironically, the most primitive and physical infrastructures—the power grid and generation facilities—are holding us back.

To overcome this bottleneck, global Big Tech companies are seeking new breakthroughs. Recently, giants like Microsoft and Amazon have begun partnering with Small Modular Reactors (SMRs) and next-generation nuclear power plants. Nuclear energy is being re-evaluated as virtually the only alternative capable of providing high-quality, stable electricity without carbon emissions. Furthermore, technologies such as High-Voltage Direct Current (HVDC) to minimize power loss during transmission, and "low-power semiconductor" designs to maximize the energy efficiency of AI chips themselves, have become key indicators determining a company’s survival.

From an economic perspective, this can be interpreted as a case where "resource scarcity" dictates the direction of technological development. If computational speed was the core metric in the past, "performance per watt" (efficiency) now dominates market logic. At the national level, expanding the power grid has transcended simple infrastructure investment to become a strategic asset determining digital competitiveness. This is because a nation, no matter how superior its software technology may be, is bound to be left behind if it lacks the "vessel"—the physical infrastructure—to implement it.

Ultimately, the success of the AI revolution depends on the flow of electricity moving invisibly behind the scenes. We must confront the importance of the solid infrastructure of the power grid, hidden behind the flashy superstructure of artificial intelligence. A technological powerhouse that fails to secure the stability and efficiency of its energy supply is nothing more than a house built on sand. The "energy war" the world is currently waging is not just a fight for resources, but a part of a grand blueprint to determine who will seize leadership in the coming future.
