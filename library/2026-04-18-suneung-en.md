### [Opinion] The New Frontier of AI and the Critical Threshold of Power Supply

The central theme of the global IT industry today is undoubtedly "Generative AI." This innovation, triggered by OpenAI’s ChatGPT, is encroaching upon the realms of creativity and reasoning, areas once considered uniquely human. However, behind this brilliant technological facade lies a massive physical challenge that humanity must resolve: the supply of electric power. As artificial intelligence becomes more sophisticated, the exponentially increasing energy consumption of data centers has evolved beyond a mere cost issue into a critical variable shaking the foundations of national energy policies.

Compared to the power consumed by a single query in a conventional search engine, the electricity required for a Large Language Model (LLM) to generate a response is approximately 10 to 30 times higher. Hyperscale data centers, where tens of thousands of GPUs (Graphics Processing Units) operate 24 hours a day, have essentially become "energy-hungry giants." The International Energy Agency (IEA) has warned that by 2026, electricity consumption from global data centers will more than double compared to 2022, reaching a level equivalent to the total electricity consumption of Japan.

A serious contradiction arises here: the conflict between "carbon neutrality" and "fostering the AI industry." Big Tech companies like Google and Microsoft have advocated for eco-friendly management by increasing their use of renewable energy. However, renewable sources such as solar and wind power are insufficient to support the "base load" of data centers, which must operate without interruption, due to the problem of intermittency caused by weather conditions. At this juncture, the breakthrough chosen by global Big Tech firms is, surprisingly, nuclear power.

Recently, Microsoft signed a contract to secure an exclusive power supply by restarting Unit 1 of the Three Mile Island nuclear plant, the site of a past accident. Similarly, Amazon and Google are staking their futures on securing energy sovereignty by making large-scale investments in Small Modular Reactor (SMR) developers. Nuclear power is being re-evaluated as the most realistic alternative in the AI era because it can provide a stable, massive supply of electricity while emitting almost no carbon.

This phenomenon provides us with significant insights. First, technological innovation never remains solely within the realm of software. Securing the physical infrastructure and energy resources to support algorithms has become the core of national competitiveness. Second, there is a necessary shift in perspective regarding the "energy mix." It is time for pragmatic reflection on which energy combination is most strategic for the survival of high-tech industries, moving away from the binary logic of "anti-nuclear versus eco-friendly."

From a reading comprehension perspective, we must understand the correlation between the phenomenon of "AI development" and the constraint of "energy supply." We must recognize that technological progress does not necessarily guarantee a rosy future and that it inevitably requires new social consensus and the restructuring of infrastructure. The true winner of the AI era may not be the company that creates the smartest model, but the one that secures the most stable power to run that model. The current situation, where the intangible intelligence of AI is merging with the tangible energy of nuclear power, is the most peculiar yet inevitable encounter in the history of modern civilization.

1. **Paradox** (역설): A situation or statement that seems to contradict itself but may be true.
2. **Intermittency** (간헐성): The quality of starting and stopping at intervals; a major challenge for renewable energy like wind and solar.
3. **Sovereignty** (주권/통제권): Supreme power or authority; in this context, the ability of a company or nation to control its own energy future.
4. **Pragmatic** (실용적인): Dealing with things sensibly and realistically in a way that is based on practical rather than theoretical considerations.
5. **Intangible** (무형의): Unable to be touched or grasped; not having a physical presence (like software or intelligence).
