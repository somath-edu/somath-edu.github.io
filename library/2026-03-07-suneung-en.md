The contemporary paradigm of the artificial intelligence (AI) industry is undergoing a radical shift, / transitioning from the mere "generation of language" / toward the more sophisticated realms of "thinking and reasoning." We have entered an era of "reasoning AI," / where large language models (LLMs) go beyond mimicking human linguistic patterns / to independently decompose and verify complex logical problems. This transition signifies more than just technical progress; / it poses fundamental questions regarding the nature of intellectual labor / and the unique domains of human thought.

Conventional generative AI operated by selecting the most probable next word / based on statistical predictions derived from vast datasets. While excellent at producing plausible responses, / these models revealed inherent limitations—often termed "hallucinations"— / when faced with mathematical proofs or high-level logical inference. In contrast, / the recently highlighted reasoning models formulate an internal "Chain of Thought" before delivering an answer. In other words, / they establish intermediate steps to resolve a problem, / scrutinize each stage for errors, / and undergo a process of finding the optimal solution.

This shift can be elucidated through the concepts of "System 1" and "System 2" / proposed by the Nobel laureate Daniel Kahneman. If previous AI corresponded to System 1, / which makes intuitive and rapid judgments, / the new reasoning models have entered the territory of System 2, / which performs slow, analytical, and logical deliberation. This suggests that AI may transcend being a mere tool for information retrieval / to assist or even replace high-level intellectual activities / such as research and development, legal analysis, and complex architectural design.

However, behind this technological leap / lie critical points that necessitate cautious scrutiny. The first is the efficiency of "inference-time compute." / As the reasoning process lengthens, / the computing power and energy consumption required by AI increase exponentially. This raises concerns that the "capital gap" between corporations / may solidify into a permanent "intelligence gap." Second is the potential atrophy of human critical thinking skills. / If humans accept sophisticated logical outputs from AI without verification, / we may face the danger of delegating our "logical authority" entirely to machines.

Ultimately, the advent of reasoning AI / demands that humans formulate "higher-level questions." If AI can navigate the path to a correct answer, / humans must focus on defining the values and purposes that those answers should serve. Technology is merely a tool, / and the rudder that determines its direction / remains firmly in human hands. More important than complex equations / is the insight to perceive the contemporary challenges / that those equations are intended to solve.
