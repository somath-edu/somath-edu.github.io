**[Feature] The Era of Answering Questions is Over... The Rise of AI That 'Thinks and Acts' Independently**

The emergence of ChatGPT, which swept the world last year, presented humanity with the massive discourse of "Generative AI." While Large Language Models (LLMs) that understand human language and provide contextually appropriate answers were revolutionary in themselves, the central axis of the IT industry is recently shifting rapidly beyond mere "answering" toward "execution." The era of the so-called "AI Agent" has arrived.

An AI agent refers to an autonomous system that establishes its own plans, utilizes necessary tools, and generates results to achieve set goals without specific, step-by-step instructions from the user. If a conventional chatbot stayed at the level of providing information in response to a question like "How is the weather in Jeju tomorrow?", an AI agent performs the actual task—up to the point of payment—when requested to "book a flight and accommodation within a budget of 500,000 won for a trip to Jeju tomorrow."

The core of this technological leap lies in the combination of "reasoning" and "tool-use capability." While early LLMs were based on predicting the next most probable word using vast amounts of data, the latest AI agents undergo a process of "self-reflection," where they divide complex tasks into sub-tasks and correct their own errors at each stage. Furthermore, by possessing interfaces that allow them to interact with external environments—such as web browsing, API calls, and software manipulation—they have secured the practical ability to act within digital environments.

From an economic perspective, the proliferation of AI agents signifies labor with "zero marginal cost." Beyond simple repetitive customer service or data analysis, companies will be able to entrust AI with the design of complex business processes. This will undoubtedly serve as a catalyst to drastically increase productivity across all industries. At the same time, however, it raises serious ethical and legal questions. Who is responsible for damages or copyright infringements caused by autonomous decisions made by an AI agent? Are there sufficient safeguards to control the agent when its actions deviate from the principal's (user's) intentions?

Let us examine this phenomenon through the lens of logical inquiry. Technological progress always involves a "trade-off" between convenience and control. As AI is delegated more authority as a human agent, the room for human intervention decreases. From a "philosophical" perspective often discussed in academic discourse, we must hasten a social consensus on whether to view AI as a mere tool or to recognize it as a subject of social action.

Ultimately, competitiveness in the era of AI agents will depend not on "who creates a smarter AI," but on "who assigns more sophisticated goals to the AI and supervises that process." In an age where the application of information and critical thinking have become more important than the simple acquisition of information, students should not settle for the "correct answers" provided by AI, but should instead cultivate the "power of questioning" to identify logical flaws in AI and exercise control over it.

**[Senior Editor’s Comment]**
The era where the volume of knowledge equaled power is fading. Now, the "designer's critical thinking"—deciding which direction to steer the powerful engine called AI—will determine success or failure in both entrance exams and the real world.
