### [Opinion] Beyond the Brilliance of Artificial Intelligence: Where Lies the Solution for the 'Hunger for Power'?

The monumental advancement of artificial intelligence (AI) technology is fundamentally reshaping the landscape of modern society. While the fluent prose and sophisticated images generated by generative AI seem poised to dramatically enhance human productivity, a critical physical limitation lies beneath the surface of this digital revolution: its extreme energy intensity. The recent phenomenon of global Big Tech companies partnering with nuclear power plants and investing astronomical sums into Small Modular Reactors (SMRs) suggests that the sustainability of the AI industry has ultimately converged upon the classical problem of "energy supply and demand."

For AI to perform learning and inference, hyper-scale data centers—where tens of thousands of Graphics Processing Units (GPUs) are connected in parallel—are essential. The problem is that these data centers consume power on a scale that defies imagination. According to a report by the International Energy Agency (IEA), the electricity consumed by a single ChatGPT query is approximately ten times higher than that of a single Google search. The power consumption of data centers worldwide has already exceeded the total consumption of many mid-sized nations and is projected to double by 2026. Data centers are not merely heavy users of electricity; they are "power-hungry giants" that must also allocate a significant portion of their energy to cooling systems to dissipate the immense heat generated during operation.

Amidst the global imperative of carbon neutrality, Big Tech companies initially focused on renewable energy sources such as solar and wind power. However, renewable energy suffers from the chronic issue of "intermittency," where power generation fluctuates depending on weather conditions. For data centers, which require a constant voltage and frequency 24 hours a day, an unstable power supply represents a fatal risk. The variability of renewable energy proved too great to handle the "base load"—the minimum level of electricity demand that must be maintained even during the lowest usage periods of the day.

In this context, Small Modular Reactors (SMRs) have emerged as a crucial solution. SMRs refer to nuclear reactors with a power generation capacity of 300MW or less, which is about one-tenth the size of conventional large-scale nuclear plants. Because components are manufactured in modules at factories and assembled on-site, construction periods are shorter and site selection is less restricted. Most importantly, their ability to provide a stable, carbon-free power supply 24/7, regardless of weather, perfectly aligns with the needs of AI companies. Recent power purchase agreements signed by Amazon and Google with SMR developers reflect the realization that securing the energy required for AI computation is directly linked to corporate survival.

Ultimately, the AI revolution is expanding beyond a competition in software to a competition in energy infrastructure. The "law of energy"—that achieving high levels of intelligence requires a corresponding physical cost—is no exception even in the digital age. We now stand at a critical juncture between the rosy future promised by AI and the massive transformation of the energy paradigm required to support it. A social consensus on the redesign of national power grids and next-generation energy sources is now more urgent than ever before.

### [오늘의 핵심 영단어]

1. **Intermittency** (간헐성): The quality of starting and stopping at intervals; a major challenge for renewable energy sources like wind and solar.
2. **Inference** (추론): In the context of AI, the process of using a trained model to make predictions or generate content.
3. **Base Load** (기저 부하): The minimum amount of electric power delivered or required over a given period at a constant rate.
4. **Dissipate** (소멸시키다/흩뜨리다): To cause to spread thin or scatter and gradually vanish; used here regarding the heat generated by data centers.
5. **Juncture** (시점/연결점): A particular point in events or time, especially a critical one where important decisions must be made.
