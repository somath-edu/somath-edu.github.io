**[Opinion] Deciphering the Structural Shifts Beneath the Wave of Ultra-Low-Priced Goods**

The Korean distribution market is currently undergoing a period of unprecedented upheaval, triggered by the aggressive offensive of Chinese e-commerce platforms, commonly referred to as “C-commerce.” These platforms, represented by AliExpress and Temu, are fundamentally shaking the consumption patterns of domestic consumers by brandishing the powerful weapon of “ultra-low prices.” This phenomenon goes beyond a simple shift in market share; it presents complex challenges to our society, such as the reorganization of global supply chains and the emergence of blind spots in regulatory frameworks.

The core engine enabling the rapid growth of C-commerce lies in the maximization of the “Direct-to-Consumer (D2C)” model. While traditional distribution structures involve multi-stage processes—manufacturing, import/export, wholesale, and retail—where margins and logistics costs are added at each step, these platforms directly connect Chinese manufacturing factories with global consumers. The cost savings generated by eliminating intermediate distribution stages are directly reflected in lower consumer prices. Combined with the Chinese government’s export incentive policies and low international postal rates, these platforms have secured a level of price competitiveness that is difficult for domestic products, or even existing direct-purchase goods, to match.

However, behind this “price destruction” lies a dark shadow of controversy regarding reverse discrimination against the domestic distribution ecosystem and consumer safety. Domestic businesses are burdened with strict quality control regulations, such as KC certification, along with various customs duties and value-added tax obligations. In contrast, C-commerce goods entering the country in the form of overseas direct purchases remain relatively free from these regulatory nets. Consequently, this leads to a narrowing of the ground for domestic small and medium-sized enterprises and acts as a factor that hinders fair competition in the retail market. Indeed, the recent detection of hazardous substances in some products has clearly exposed the limits of the platforms’ responsibility and domestic regulatory authority.

What warrants even more attention is that these players are making massive investments in building logistics systems, moving beyond merely selling low-priced goods. To overcome “slow delivery,” which was the biggest drawback of overseas direct purchases in the past, they are strengthening localization strategies, such as pushing for the construction of large-scale logistics centers within Korea. This is also directly linked to the shift in data power. We must not overlook the issue of data sovereignty, which arises as domestic consumers’ purchase patterns, preferences, and personal information are accumulated on the servers of foreign platforms.

Ultimately, the rise of C-commerce presents us with two tasks simultaneously. Consumers need a critical eye for rational consumption and safety, while the government and industry are required to fundamentally improve their structures by enhancing regulatory equity and advancing services. If the shift in the market is inevitable, it is time to devise wise countermeasures so that such changes can be established as a virtuous cycle in our economy. Insight that pierces through the dynamics of the distribution structure hidden behind the numbers on price tags is more desperate than ever.
