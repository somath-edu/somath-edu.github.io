## [Opinion] From ‘Instruments’ to ‘Agents’: Reshaping the Landscape of Industry and Logic

The focal point of the global IT industry is rapidly shifting beyond mere "Generative AI" toward "Agentic AI," which is characterized by its capacity to judge and act independently. While AI has thus far functioned as a "sophisticated encyclopedia" that provides answers to user inquiries, Agentic AI aims to be an "autonomous agent" that establishes its own plans, selects optimal tools, and executes tasks to achieve a given objective. This paradigm shift signifies more than just technical progress; it foretells a structural transformation in human labor patterns and the digital ecosystem.

The core mechanism of Agentic AI lies in "iterative reasoning." Traditional Large Language Models (LLMs) have primarily relied on a "zero-shot" approach, where they generate output through a single computation in response to an input. In contrast, Agentic AI undergoes a feedback loop in which it decomposes a goal into detailed sub-tasks and self-reviews its own results at each stage. For instance, while conventional AI might simply list general information when asked to "plan a trip to Europe," Agentic AI would search for real-time flights, book accommodations according to a budget, and calculate the efficiency of travel routes to finalize the most optimal plan.

In this process, the concept of "tool-use" capability emerges as a crucial element. Agentic AI does not rely solely on its internal knowledge; instead, it freely utilizes external APIs, web browsers, and data analysis tools. This is analogous to a human reaching for a calculator or visiting a library to find information. As AI becomes capable of performing physical or logical actions within a digital environment, corporations have reached a turning point where they can automate not only simple repetitive tasks but also entire processes requiring high-level judgment, such as supply chain management, financial analysis, and customer service.

However, this technological leap inevitably entails philosophical and ethical questions. First is the issue of the "locus of responsibility." When an autonomous decision made by an AI agent leads to unforeseen economic losses or social disruption, legal discussions are urgently needed to determine whether the responsibility lies with the developer, the user, or the algorithm itself. Second is the establishment of the scope of "human-in-the-loop" control. As human intervention is minimized to maximize efficiency, the opacity of the system increases, leading to the so-called "black box" problem.

Ultimately, the core competency in the era of Agentic AI will not be the technology itself, but rather "human literacy" and "ethical guidelines" to critically accept and coordinate autonomous AI judgments. Whether technology remains a surrogate that expands human capabilities or becomes an uncontrollable variable depends on how deeply we understand its logical structure and how effectively we design appropriate safeguards. We have reached a point where we must move beyond being passive consumers of information and adopt the attitude of "strategic designers" who command powerful artificial intelligence agents.
