### [Opinion] From Information Retrieval to Execution: The Emergence of AI Agents

The most prominent discourse in the contemporary IT industry is, without a doubt, the "AI Agent." Only a year or two ago, the public was astonished by the advent of chatbots capable of generating natural responses to human inquiries. However, the technological trajectory is now rapidly shifting beyond mere "answering" toward an autonomous stage of "action," where AI perceives user intent and executes tasks independently. This signifies not just a sophisticated upgrade of services, but a fundamental transformation in the way humans and computers interact.

While traditional Large Language Models (LLMs) served as "inference machines" that arranged the most statistically appropriate words based on vast datasets, AI agents represent a fusion of this reasoning with "tool-use capabilities" and "autonomy." For instance, if a user previously asked an AI to "plan a trip to Jeju Island," the system would limit itself to listing optimal routes and restaurant information in text. Conversely, an AI agent independently manages the entire process—booking actual flights based on the user's budget and preferences, processing hotel payments, and adjusting schedules in real-time according to weather changes.

The core engine enabling this transition is the "Agentic Workflow." This refers to an iterative cognitive process in which the AI, when confronted with a complex challenge, decomposes it into smaller task units and reviews and refines the outputs of each stage. This process is analogous to how humans draft an outline, conduct research, and revise a manuscript when writing a report. During this sequence, the AI exerts influence on the physical environment by calling external APIs (Application Programming Interfaces) to access real-time data or directly controlling other software.

From an economic perspective, the proliferation of AI agents is expected to foster a new ecosystem termed the "Agent Economy." Corporations can pursue extreme cost reduction and efficiency by delegating not only repetitive, standardized tasks but also domains requiring complex decision-making to AI. Simultaneously, however, this accelerates the structural reorganization of the labor market and brings forth ethical and legal dilemmas, such as the accountability for AI-driven decisions and the granting of excessive access to personal information.

Ultimately, what human competencies are required in the era of AI agents? In an age where AI contemplates the "methodology" for execution, humans must formulate a more acute "purpose." Paradoxically, as AI gains autonomy as a tool, human insight—the ability to critically review results and render value judgments—becomes increasingly indispensable. We stand at the threshold of a new evolution where, beyond technology serving as a mere secretary, human intelligence extends its reach directly into the digital environment. In this massive tide, what we must focus on is not the brilliance of the technology itself, but the fundamental question of how its final destination will redefine human life.
