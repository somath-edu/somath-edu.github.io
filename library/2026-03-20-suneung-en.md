## [Opinion] The Era of AI Agents: The End of Search and the Beginning of Execution

The central theme of the current information technology (IT) industry is, without a doubt, the "AI Agent." While the Large Language Model (LLM) based chatbots we have experienced so far functioned as "repositories of knowledge" by answering user questions, AI agents are evolving into "active entities" that understand user intent to plan and execute complex tasks autonomously. This signifies more than just the addition of a convenient tool; it foretells a fundamental paradigm shift in the way humans and computers interact.

The core driving force of AI agents lies in the combination of "autonomy" and "reasoning capabilities." Unlike traditional generative AI, which probabilistically predicts the next most appropriate word based on input text, agent models logically decompose a goal into necessary sub-tasks. For instance, if given a command to "book the cheapest flight to Jeju Island for tomorrow’s business trip and reserve a nearby hotel," the AI agent independently judges and performs a multi-step process, including accessing airline sites, comparing prices, checking schedules, and integrating with payment systems. It even possesses the ability to respond in real-time to variables that occur during this process.

From an economic perspective, this shift represents a "drastic reduction in transaction costs." In the past, enormous amounts of human cognitive resources and time were invested in the process of searching for information, comparing options, and making final decisions. However, as AI agents take over this process, consumer selection costs will decrease, and market efficiency is expected to be maximized. Especially in corporate environments, agents are anticipated to lead a leap in labor productivity by penetrating areas that require high-level judgment, such as data analysis, report writing, and customer service, moving beyond simple repetitive tasks.

However, there are not only rosy outlooks. The proliferation of AI agents inevitably brings about ethical and legal challenges regarding the "locus of responsibility." When an autonomous decision made by an agent infringes upon the rights of others or causes financial loss, does the responsibility lie with the developer or the user who issued the command? Furthermore, "confirmation bias" and "algorithmic opacity," which may occur as agents learn human preferences, raise concerns about undermining democratic values.

From the perspective of reading comprehension, we must interpret the phenomenon of AI agents through two axes. One is the axis of "efficiency," where technology replaces human intellectual labor, and the other is the axis of "relationality," concerning how technology will coexist with human agency. Competitiveness in future society will depend not simply on knowing a lot of information, but on how one can design and control these intelligent systems and critically accept their outcomes.

Ultimately, AI agents should be viewed not as a means to replace humans, but as powerful "intellectual amplifiers" to expand human capabilities. Depending on how we define this technological turning point and what rules we establish, AI can remain a simple secretary or become a partner leading human potential to a new dimension. Now is the time to keenly perceive the logical structures and social implications flowing beneath the surface, rather than merely marveling at the flashy appearance of technology.
