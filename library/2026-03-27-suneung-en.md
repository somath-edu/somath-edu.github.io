**[Analysis] The Evolution of Generative AI: From 'Speaking' to 'Working' — The Rise of AI Agents**

The current discourse in the information technology (IT) industry is centered on a significant paradigm shift / from 'Generative AI' to 'AI Agents.' Large Language Models (LLMs) are moving beyond the stage / where they simply generate answers to user questions / and are now evolving into active entities / that set goals, utilize tools, and complete complex tasks autonomously. This shift is heralding a fundamental transformation / not only in the way humans interact with technology / but also in the overall structure of industries.

While traditional chatbot-style AI served as a 'passive interface' / that produced optimal text based on input data, / AI agents are closer to 'autonomous executors' / that understand a user’s intent and establish and carry out execution plans. For example, / if a user asks to “plan a family trip to Jeju Island,” / a traditional AI would merely provide a list of recommended attractions. In contrast, / an AI agent considers the user’s budget and preferences, / books flight tickets, compares accommodation prices to facilitate payment, / and even suggests alternative itineraries / based on changes in the weather.

The core driver of this rapid advancement / is the combination of 'reasoning' and 'tool use' capabilities. AI agents break down complex goals into smaller subtasks / and directly call upon external software or APIs / needed at each stage. This is the result of implementing a sophisticated cognitive process / that goes beyond simply retrieving knowledge / to understanding logical sequences and allocating available resources. Experts evaluate this development / as AI gaining 'hands and feet' in addition to its 'brain.'

However, behind these optimistic technological prospects / lie numerous challenges that must be addressed. The most critical issues / are 'reliability' and 'control.' Errors (hallucinations) occurring during the process / where AI independently makes decisions and executes them / can lead to actual economic losses or security incidents, / unlike simple text errors. Furthermore, the reorganization of the labor market / and the ambiguity of responsibility arising as AI agents replace human tasks / are areas that require social consensus.

Ultimately, the era of AI agents demands new competencies from humans. Rather than technical proficiency in producing or processing information, / the ability of humans to act as 'supervisors' /—presenting correct goals to AI agents, critically reviewing the process, and making final value judgments—/ will become increasingly important. Beyond the era where technology is a human assistant / to an era where it becomes a colleague, / we must prepare insightful answers / on how to coexist with this proactive technology.
