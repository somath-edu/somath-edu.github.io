**[Analysis] Data Oversaturation, the Crisis of Trust, and the Reconfiguration of Literacy in the AI Era**

The most prominent discourse currently permeating the information technology (IT) industry and society at large is undoubtedly the explosive proliferation of "Generative AI." In the two short years since the emergence of ChatGPT, artificial intelligence has advanced beyond simple text generation to a level where it perfectly replicates images, videos, and even human voices. This technological progress, however, paradoxically imposes an unprecedented cost on society: the burden of discerning the "authenticity" of the information we consume.

In the past, the production of information was predicated on human intellectual labor. To write a single newspaper article or an academic paper, immense time, research, and logical verification processes were essential. Generative AI, however, constructs plausible sentences and arguments in mere seconds based on Large Language Models (LLMs). The problem lies in the fact that when AI generates information, it relies on "probabilistic likelihood" rather than "veracity." This leads to the so-called "hallucination" phenomenon, resulting in a flood of "synthetic media" where fact and fiction are sophisticatedly intertwined.

From an economic perspective, this phenomenon exacerbates information asymmetry and accelerates the transformation of the information ecosystem into a "Lemon Market." This logic suggests that as it becomes harder for consumers to distinguish between high-quality and low-quality information, only the latter remains in the market. Already, fake news and manipulated images generated by AI are overflowing on the internet, distorting the process of public opinion formation and destroying the public good of social trust. The fear of the "Dead Internet Theory"—the idea that most of the internet will be filled by bots and AI rather than humans—is now emerging as a realistic warning.

In response, movements at technical and institutional levels are gaining momentum. Systems such as "digital watermarks" for AI-generated content or "Content Credentials" that track the source of information using blockchain technology are being introduced. Nevertheless, what is more fundamentally required than technical safeguards is the "critical literacy" of information consumers. 

Much like navigating the complex non-fiction passages of a high-stakes academic exam, we now live in an era where we must constantly question the author’s intent and the validity of arguments whenever we encounter a text. As the volume of information increases exponentially, human discernment—the ability to judge the value of that information—ironically becomes the most scarce resource. Reading comprehension in the age of Generative AI must go beyond simply reading and understanding words; it must be a high-level cognitive operation that reads the shadows of algorithms hidden behind the text and identifies fragments of truth.

Ultimately, the question of whether technological advancement will replace humans boils down to how prepared we are to view the world with sharper insight. This is because the final bastion preventing the pollution of the information ecosystem lies not in algorithms, but in the sharp "power of reading" that penetrates the logical loopholes of a text.
