The most prominent topic in the current IT industry is the rapid emergence of conversational AI search services, such as OpenAI’s "SearchGPT" and "Perplexity." Google’s "PageRank" algorithm, which has dominated the global search market for the past two decades, is now facing a significant challenge. This transition is regarded as a "paradigm shift" that fundamentally transforms not only technological progress but also the way humanity consumes information and the underlying business models of the web ecosystem.

The core mechanism of traditional search engines is "indexing" and "listing." When a user enters a keyword, the search engine crawls web pages worldwide and displays relevant links according to their priority. In this process, the user functions as an active agent who selects and synthesizes information by directly clicking on various links. However, AI search engines omit this step. Models that have pre-trained on vast datasets aggregate real-time web information and directly generate "complete answers" to the user's queries. In other words, the definition of search has shifted from a "tool for finding" to an "entity that provides answers."

While this change maximizes the efficiency of information retrieval, it simultaneously threatens the "virtuous cycle of traffic," which is the foundation of the web ecosystem. Currently, most news organizations and content creators generate advertising revenue through visitors flowing into their websites. However, if the "zero-click" phenomenon—where AI summarizes the content and displays it entirely within the search result window—intensifies, the inflow to original sites will inevitably plummet. This leads to a decline in creators' revenue, which may eventually cause the "paradox of data depletion," where the supply of high-quality data is cut off, thereby degrading the performance of AI models themselves.

Furthermore, the inherent limitations of AI search, such as "hallucination" and the intensification of "confirmation bias," cannot be overlooked. Because AI generates the most statistically probable sentences, it can smoothly describe incorrect facts as if they were the truth. If users blindly trust AI's answers without verifying the original links, the distortion of information will spread uncontrollably. Moreover, if AI provides only customized answers tailored to a user's preferences, the "filter bubble" phenomenon—where individuals see only what they want to see—is likely to be reinforced, solidifying social confirmation bias.

Ultimately, the era of AI search demands a new form of "media literacy." The ability to critically examine the source and logical validity of information hidden behind technological convenience is more important than ever. Amidst the changing dynamics of technological power, we stand at a crossroads: will we completely surrender our search sovereignty to AI, or will we utilize AI as a supplementary tool for critical thinking? In an age where artificial intelligence provides the answers, humans must be able to ask even sharper "questions."
