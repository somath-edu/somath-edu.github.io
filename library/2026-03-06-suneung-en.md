# [Editorial] The Evolutionary Leap of AI: From ‘Verbal Assistants’ to ‘Autonomous Agents’

The most prominent buzzword in the recent Information Technology (IT) industry is, without a doubt, the "AI Agent." The generative AI craze, which began just two years ago with the emergence of ChatGPT, is now moving beyond the stage of "language models" that simply answer human questions. It is evolving into "acting entities" that make independent judgments and complete complex tasks. This shift heralds a fundamental transformation not only in technological advancement but also in the overall structure of human labor and the digital ecosystem.

While traditional chatbots focused on generating contextually appropriate text based on Large Language Models (LLMs), AI agents are characterized by "autonomy" and "executability." After understanding a user’s abstract goal, an agent independently establishes a detailed plan to achieve it. For instance, if given a command like "Book my business trip for next week," the agent checks the user’s calendar, searches for flights and hotels, and completes the final reservation by linking payment methods. In this process, the AI does not merely act as a messenger providing information but performs the role of an agent that produces tangible results by utilizing external tools.

This evolution is made possible because the "reasoning" capabilities of AI have been significantly strengthened. Latest models are adopting "Chain of Thought" techniques, which involve reviewing a series of thought processes step-by-step to solve problems. This is similar to the way humans follow logical steps when solving complex issues. Furthermore, as the ability to interact with external environments—such as web browsers, emails, and databases—through APIs (Application Programming Interfaces) is combined, the scope of AI activity is expanding beyond the virtual world into the realm of real-world tasks.

From an economic perspective, the proliferation of AI agents implies the supply of intelligent labor at a "near-zero marginal cost." While this can drastically increase corporate productivity, it simultaneously poses serious challenges to the white-collar labor market. This is because the possibility of AI replacing not only simple repetitive administrative tasks but also intermediate-level work requiring a certain degree of judgment has increased. Moreover, the issue of "accountability" for malfunctions or unexpected results occurring during the AI's autonomous decision-making process is a matter of social consensus that must be addressed urgently.

Ultimately, the arrival of the AI agent era demands new competencies from humans. Rather than individual skills like searching for information or writing documents, humans must now possess critical thinking skills as "orchestra conductors" who assign clear goals to AI and oversee the ethics and efficiency of the process. In an era where technology acts on behalf of humans, the subject of essential value judgment—deciding "for what purpose we should act"—must remain exclusively human.
