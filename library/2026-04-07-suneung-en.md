### [Editorial] The Evolution of AI: From an Informational Tool to an Independent Proxy

The current discourse in the global IT industry has shifted beyond mere "generative AI" toward the emergence of "AI Agents." While previous artificial intelligence served primarily as an "informational summarizer" that provided answers to user inquiries, we are now entering an era of "proxies" that comprehend a user’s ultimate purpose and autonomously execute tasks to completion. This transition signifies more than just technical progress; it foreshadows a fundamental rupture in human economic activities and cognitive processes.

Conventional search engines and early chatbots were confined to listing relevant information based on user-inputted keywords or generating refined sentences. In other words, the subject of final judgment and execution was always the human user. However, AI agents integrate "reasoning" with "tool-utilization capabilities." For instance, if a user commands, "Book a trip to Jeju Island for next week," the AI analyzes the user’s preferences, compares accommodations and flights, and proceeds through the operational stages up to the point of final payment. In this process, the AI demonstrates autonomy by performing "task decomposition"—breaking down complex goals into sub-tasks—and recalibrating its strategy when unexpected variables arise.

From an economic perspective, the proliferation of AI agents extends the concept of "zero marginal cost" from information production to the realm of service execution. Although this brings a dramatic surge in productivity, it simultaneously entails the risk of "cognitive outsourcing." When humans delegate complex decision-making processes entirely to AI, their capacity to structure problems and think critically is likely to undergo atrophy. Furthermore, we cannot overlook the "responsibility gap," where the legal and ethical accountability for the outcomes produced by AI remains ambiguous.

Ultimately, the core competency required of humans in the age of AI agents is not the "art of commanding," but rather the "insight for value judgment." As AI perfectly resolves the question of "how" to perform a task, humans must focus more intensely on the essential questions of "what" should be done and "why." Whether technology remains a helpful proxy that alleviates human labor or becomes an encroaching ruler that erodes human agency depends on the institutional safeguards we design and our attitude toward critical acceptance.

Today’s readers must cultivate an "aesthetic eye" and "critical literacy" to discern what is true and valuable among the numerous results produced by AI. In an era where technological velocity outpaces philosophical reflection, we stand at a crossroads where we must once again ask ourselves: what is the true essence of being human?
