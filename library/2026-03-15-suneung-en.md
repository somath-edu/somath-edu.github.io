### [Editorial] The Rise of AI Search and the Paradigm Shift in the Knowledge Ecosystem

For the past two decades, "searching"—the act of locating desired information within a vast sea of data—has served as the core mechanism for humanity to navigate the digital world. However, the "era of search engines," which listed numerous webpages and required users to select the optimal information themselves, is fading. In its place, the "era of conversational search" is arriving, where artificial intelligence (AI) identifies the intent behind a query and provides a single, comprehensive answer. Recent announcements, such as OpenAI’s "SearchGPT" and the rapid growth of AI startups like Perplexity, are accelerating this transformation. Yet, behind this innovative convenience lies a structural contradiction that could undermine the very foundation of the knowledge ecosystem.

Traditional search engines operate on the logic of "indexing" and "ranking." When a search term is entered, the engine provides links to highly relevant pages from its database, directing users to those specific websites. In this process, website operators gain advertising revenue or public recognition, which in turn acts as a driving force for producing high-quality content. This has been the virtuous cycle of the "Open Web" that has been maintained for decades.

In contrast, AI search is centered on "Retrieval-Augmented Generation" (RAG) technology. This involves Large Language Models (LLMs) that have processed vast amounts of data, searching for real-time information to provide summarized answers directly to the user. While this efficiency eliminates the inconvenience of clicking multiple links to compare information, it paradoxically threatens the survival of knowledge providers. As AI absorbs the core content of original sources to deliver its answers, the "zero-click" phenomenon—where users no longer have an incentive to visit original websites—is intensifying.

Here, we witness a conflict between cognitive convenience and the sustainability of the ecosystem. For AI search models to learn from and reference high-quality data, the economic foundation of the human creators who produce that data must paradoxically be maintained. If AI processes and distributes data without authorization, thereby destroying the revenue structure of original creators, it could lead to the "desertification of information," where the "new information" for AI to learn eventually dries up. This transcends a simple issue of copyright infringement; it leads to a social loss where the incentive system for knowledge production itself collapses.

Ultimately, the evolution of AI search depends as much on establishing a fair distribution model as it does on achieving technical perfection. Recent moves by some AI companies to sign paid news supply agreements with media outlets reflect a desperate awareness of this issue. However, it remains uncertain whether revenue distribution centered on giant platforms can fairly reach small-scale creators or academic data producers.

Students should pay close attention to the "costs" hidden under the name of "innovation." Technology has always evolved to increase human convenience, but when it destroys the soil upon which that convenience is built, it faces its own limitations. For AI search to become an "expansion of knowledge" rather than a "monopoly of answers," a new digital contract is urgently needed—one that allows all parties contributing to value creation to coexist. We must not forget that behind a single, clear AI response lies someone’s intense thought and labor.

**[Insight from the Senior Editor]**
"When the efficiency of technology overwhelms the motivation for production, the progress of knowledge stops. The completion of AI search lies not in the sophistication of algorithms, but in establishing the ethics of coexistence."
