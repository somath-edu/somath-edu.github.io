### [Tech & Economy] Beyond Talking AI: The Rise of Agentic Systems and Their Implications

Recent discourse in the artificial intelligence (AI) industry is shifting its focus from mere "chatbots" that respond to queries to "AI agents" capable of autonomous planning and execution. While generative AI had largely remained in the stage of "reasoning"—producing text and images based on patterns—since the debut of ChatGPT in 2022, it is now evolving into the stage of "action." In this new phase, AI interacts with the external environment and utilizes various tools to achieve complex goals without constant human intervention. This shift signifies a fundamental transformation not only in technological progress but also in economic structures and the nature of human labor.

The primary distinction between AI agents and traditional Large Language Models (LLMs) lies in "autonomy" and "goal-orientation." Traditional LLMs are essentially passive entities that generate optimal responses based on user prompts and pre-trained data. In contrast, AI agents possess the ability to decompose a complex objective into several sub-tasks and determine the most efficient sequence of execution. For instance, when given a command to "organize next week’s business trip and book flights and accommodation," an agent can independently check the user's calendar, access booking websites, and even finalize payments.

This sophisticated mechanism is made possible by the integration of three core elements: planning, memory, and tool use. To solve problems, agents remember past experiences or contexts and utilize external tools, such as web browsers, calculators, or APIs, at the appropriate moment. Notably, through a process called "self-reflection," agents repeatedly evaluate the results of their own actions and correct errors, thereby increasing the probability of success. This indicates that AI is transcending its role as a digital assistant to become an independent unit of task performance.

From an economic perspective, the proliferation of AI agents is akin to supplying labor with "zero marginal cost." Corporations can expect explosive productivity gains by delegating repetitive and complex workflows—such as customer service, software coding, and data analysis—to these agents. However, this evolution simultaneously creates social tension, as significant portions of white-collar jobs that once required high levels of expertise may now be subject to automation. Furthermore, urgent challenges have emerged, including the issue of "AI's legal personhood" regarding liability for autonomous transactions and the potential for misuse through security vulnerabilities.

Ultimately, the dawn of the AI agent era compels us to prioritize the value of "planning" and "supervision" over "execution." As AI becomes the primary user of tools, humans will be required to cultivate capabilities as "orchestrators" who review the ethical validity of AI-driven decisions and set the overall strategic direction. Whether this technological evolution diminishes the human role or unlocks a new dimension of creativity depends entirely on the institutional frameworks we establish to manage and coexist with these intelligent agents.
