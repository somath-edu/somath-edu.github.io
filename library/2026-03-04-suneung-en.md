## [Special Feature] The Paradigm Shift in AI: Transitioning from 'Chatbots' to 'Agents'

Recently, the central focus of the global IT industry has undeniably shifted toward "AI Agents." While the generative AI that surprised the public over the past two years remained at the level of a "language model" responding to user inquiries, it is now evolving into an "execution entity" that identifies user intentions and autonomously performs complex tasks. This transition is projected to go beyond mere technical progress and fundamentally reshape the entirety of human digital experiences and industrial structures.

While conventional chatbots focused on generating contextually appropriate text based on Large Language Models (LLMs), AI agents integrate "reasoning" and "tool-use" capabilities. The core mechanism of an agent begins with the "planning" stage, where it breaks down a user’s ambiguous command into detailed sub-steps. For instance, upon receiving a command such as "Book a family trip to Jeju Island for this weekend," the agent independently judges and executes a series of processes, including searching flight schedules, checking accommodation availability, and reflecting the preferences of family members.

The background that has enabled this technological leap is the "computer use" capability of artificial intelligence. Latest AI models have begun to manipulate software directly at the operating system (OS) level by learning how humans view screens, click mice, and type. This is significant in that the AI serves as a bridge to resolve the issue of "data fragmentation," which occurred because individual applications were not interconnected.

From an economic perspective, the emergence of AI agents heralds the dawn of an era of "hyper-productivity." Enterprises can maximize the efficiency of human resources by delegating simple, repetitive tasks to agents. However, concerns regarding the rapid transformation of the white-collar labor market coexist. As standardized tasks such as data entry, scheduling, and customer service are replaced by agents, "meta-cognitive" skills—the ability for humans to supervise AI and make high-level creative decisions—are expected to become increasingly vital.

Ethical and security issues also cannot be overlooked. As AI agents gain direct access to users' personal information or payment methods, the responsibility for accountability in the event of a security breach may become blurred. Furthermore, securing "alignment" technology—ensuring that autonomous decisions made by agents do not violate human values or safety guidelines—will be a key variable determining the success or failure of the future AI industry.

Ultimately, the advent of the AI agent era signifies that artificial intelligence is being elevated from a mere "secretary" to a "proxy" for humans. We stand at a critical crossroads where we must enjoy the convenience provided by AI, while simultaneously reflecting critically on the social repercussions of technological autonomy and establishing relevant institutional frameworks.
