**[Current Issue Analysis] The Rise of AI Agents and the Double-Edged Sword of Autonomous Systems**

The current discourse in the information technology (IT) industry has shifted significantly from mere "generative AI" toward the advent of "AI Agents." For the past few years, the public has been enthralled by chatbots that answer questions and generate images. However, the technological trajectory is now rapidly moving beyond simple tools that follow human commands toward "autonomous subjects" that set their own goals, design execution steps, and utilize external tools to complete complex tasks.

While traditional Large Language Models (LLMs) served as "reasoning engines" that statistically predicted the most appropriate next word for a given input, AI agents combine this reasoning with the capabilities of "action" and "planning." For instance, if a user requests a European travel plan, whereas conventional AI would stop at merely drafting an itinerary, an agentic AI aims to verify the user’s budget, compare flight prices, and autonomously execute the actual booking and payment process. This implies that AI has become capable of directly manipulating operating systems or web browsers in a digital environment, much like a human does.

The core drivers of this transformation lie in the "Chain of Thought" and "tool-use capability." To solve complex problems, an agent breaks them down into smaller sub-tasks and calls upon necessary external APIs or software at each stage. If an error occurs during execution, the agent activates a feedback loop to analyze the cause and revise its strategy. This "self-reflective" attribute is elevating AI from a simple information retrieval tool to a practical alternative for human labor.

From an economic perspective, the proliferation of AI agents heralds a radical improvement in productivity. The contribution of agents is expected to grow sharply not only in repetitive administrative tasks but also in high-skilled domains such as software development and data analysis. However, this simultaneously raises fundamental questions regarding "human value." Concerns are being voiced that as AI takes the initiative in judgment and execution, ethical issues regarding accountability arise, and the authority of "decision-making," once considered a uniquely human domain, may be diminished.

Furthermore, the arrival of the "Agent Economy" could shake the foundations of existing platform business models. Instead of users visiting websites directly to view advertisements and find information, AI agents may handle all communications behind the scenes, potentially leading to the collapse of the click-based advertising market. This represents a significant inflection point that could lead to a restructuring of information distribution and wealth.

Ultimately, the competencies required in the era of AI agents are, paradoxically, "critical reception" and "problem-definition skills." While AI solves the "how" of a task, humans must possess sharper insights into "what" should be done and "why." As technology replaces human actions, we must solidify our position as "wise observers" who monitor and coordinate the direction in which that technology moves.
