## [Editorial] AI Agents: The Evolution from 'Tools' to 'Delegates' and a Critical Reflection

The recent focal point of the IT industry is the emergence of "AI Agents" that transcend simple conversational artificial intelligence. While previous generative AI remained in the role of an "information provider" answering user questions, it is now evolving into an "action-oriented delegate" that understands user goals, establishes plans independently, and completes execution. This shift heralds a massive paradigm change that shakes the very foundation of interfaces—the way humans and computers interact.

The core mechanisms of AI agents can be summarized into three main components. First is **"Reasoning and Planning."** When a user gives an abstract command such as "Book a family trip to Jeju Island for next week," the agent decomposes this into detailed tasks—booking flights, selecting accommodations, and establishing an itinerary—and determines their logical sequence. Second is **"Tool Use."** The agent performs practical tasks by linking with external software, such as operating a web browser or accessing payment systems on its own. Third is **"Memory."** It derives personalized results by storing the user’s past preferences and the context of previous conversations in short-term and long-term memory devices.

This technical progress is positive in that it maximizes economic efficiency. This is because it can drastically reduce the "learning costs" that humans once invested to master complex software and the "time costs" spent on repetitive tasks. Now, the interface is completely transitioning from the visual-centered GUI (Graphic User Interface) to the language-centered LUI (Language User Interface), and devices are being elevated from passive tools that mediate human intent to autonomous agents of execution.

However, behind the benefits of technology, sharp critical issues exist. First is the problem of **"the locus of responsibility."** When an AI agent causes a "hallucination" during the execution process, leading to an incorrect reservation or financial loss, the legal and ethical standards regarding whether the responsibility lies with the developer, the user, or the algorithm itself remain ambiguous. Furthermore, there are deep concerns about the phenomenon of **"cognitive outsourcing,"** where human independent judgment deteriorates as agents handle all decision-making.

Ultimately, the competency required in the era of AI agents is not blind dependence on technology, but rather "meta-cognition"—the ability to verify and critically accept the results produced by technology. We must constantly ask ourselves whether artificial intelligence is our "tool," our "delegate," or a "ruler" that encroaches upon our "will." At this moment, when the speed of technology outpaces that of ethics and institutions, we stand at a point where we must be wary of the potential loss of autonomy hidden behind the sweet fruit of convenience.
