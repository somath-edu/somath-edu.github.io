**[Lead] Moving beyond simple conversation, "AI Agents" that grasp user intent and execute tasks independently are emerging as a focal point in the IT industry, signaling a fundamental paradigm shift in how humans and computers interact.**

The current trajectory of artificial intelligence (AI) technology is rapidly shifting from the stage of "chatbots" based on Large Language Models (LLMs) to the era of "Agentic AI," which autonomously performs complex user commands. While conventional generative AI remained a "passive assistant" that generated responses to queries, AI agents take the form of "active executors" that formulate plans to achieve goals, select necessary tools, and refine their processes based on execution outcomes.

The core competencies of AI agents can be summarized into three primary dimensions. First is "reasoning and planning." If a user commands, "Plan a family trip to Jeju Island for next week and book the flights and accommodations," the AI decomposes this into detailed sub-tasks and determines the order of execution. Second is "tool utilization." It performs actual physical and digital tasks by interlocking with external software such as web browsers, payment systems, and email. Third is "memory and feedback." By learning from past task data and user preferences, it derives personalized optimal results and proposes self-corrections when errors occur.

This shift is projected to have a profound impact on economic structures and industrial ecosystems. From a corporate perspective, productivity can be drastically enhanced by automating repetitive work processes. Especially in fields such as software development, data analysis, and customer service, the deployment of AI agents will act as a catalyst for restructuring labor-intensive frameworks into technology-intensive ones. Simultaneously, however, one cannot rule out the possibility of a rapid contraction in the white-collar labor market and social controversy regarding the definition of professional proficiency.

Along with technical progress, ethical and security challenges remain significant. As AI agents gain direct access to users' personal information or payment methods, issues regarding the locus of responsibility for data breaches or malfunctions arise. If an AI misinterprets a user’s intent and proceeds with an unauthorized payment or sends an inappropriate email, a clear social consensus is still lacking on whether the legal liability lies with the developer or the user.

Ultimately, the advent of the AI agent era demands new capabilities from humans. Beyond merely memorizing knowledge or operating tools, "high-level planning skills"—the ability to present precise goals to AI, critically review the results, and exercise ethical control—will become paramount. Now that artificial intelligence has begun to possess agency beyond being a mere tool, we must gain insight into the structural changes hidden behind technological convenience and question once again what unique values only humans can possess.

**[Chief Editor's Comment]**
"AI agents will alleviate human 'labor,' but they will simultaneously put human 'thinking' on trial. To avoid becoming slaves to technology, we must learn how to verify before we learn how to command."
